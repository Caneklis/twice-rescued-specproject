The bronze portrait of Francesco del Nero (1487-1563), a member of a rich Florentine family and a treasurer of pope Clement VII, was acquired by the museums of Berlin in Florence in 1895 in Palazzo Torrigiani (formerly Palazzo del Nero) with the help of the Florentine antiquarian Stefano Bardini. The bust closely repeats a marble portrait from Francesco del Nero’s tomb in a Roman church of Santa Maria sopra Minerva made by Giulio Mazzoni, a pupil of the more famous Daniele da Volterra. According to Wilhelm von Bode (1896), the bronze version was a prototype by Mazzoni, and the marble one was merely a copy after it. This conclusion was challenged by Vasily Rastorguev (2018), who proposed that the present work to be a late-19th century copy after the Roman bust.

Conservation

The metal composition was determined. The surface was cleaned and consolidated. Surface losses were partially filled. The bust was mounted onto a support frame on a marble pedestal.
